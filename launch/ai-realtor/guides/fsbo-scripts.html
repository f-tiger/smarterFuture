FSBO Scripts: How to Win Over For-Sale-By-Owner Sellers

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

A for-sale-by-owner seller has told the world two things: they want to sell, and they don't (yet) want to pay an agent. Arguing with the second point on day one is how you lose. Helping them succeed — until the day they'd rather have help — is how you win. Roughly four in five FSBOs eventually list with an agent; your job is to be the obvious choice when they do.

The first call: permission, not pitch

  • "Hi [First name], this is [Your name] with [Brokerage]. I saw your home on [Street] — first off, it shows really well. Quick honest question: are you open to working with an agent, or are you committed to selling it yourself? Either answer is fine, I just don't want to waste your time."

If they say "selling it ourselves," don't hang up — deliver the line that separates you from every other caller:

  • "I respect that. Here's my offer with no strings: I'll send you my seller checklist — the disclosure forms people forget, the photo mistakes that cost money, and how to screen buyers so you're not opening the door to strangers. If you sell it yourself with my checklist, genuinely, congratulations. If you ever want backup, you'll know one agent who helped before asking for anything."

Why this works

Every FSBO expects agents to say "you'll fail without me." By betting on their success, you're memorable, and you've earned the right to check in. The checklist costs you nothing — and every follow-up now has a natural excuse.

The follow-up cadence (this is where listings are won)

  • Week 1: Send the checklist same day. Text two days later: "Did the checklist land okay? One thing I'd add for your street specifically: [one real tip]."
  • Week 2: "How was the weekend traffic? If you're getting lookers but no offers, it's usually price or photos — happy to give you an honest read, free."
  • Week 3+: "Quick market note: [comp] just went under contract at [price]. Useful data point for your pricing either way."

You're never asking for the listing. You're being useful on a schedule. When showings dry up — and for most FSBOs they do — you're the only agent they can hire without feeling like they're admitting defeat to a stranger.

Objections you'll hear

  • "We don't want to pay a commission." — "Nobody does — you want the number you walk away with to be bigger. FSBOs typically sell for meaningfully less than agented homes, which is why I'd rather show you the math for your house than argue percentages. Can I run it? Takes ten minutes, and if the math doesn't work in your favor with me, I'll tell you."
  • "We already have a buyer." — "That's great — a third of FSBO sales are to someone the owner already knew. Make sure the contract and disclosures protect you; that's the part that goes wrong. If you want, I'll do a one-time contract review for a flat fee instead of a full listing."
  • "Agents just want to list us." — "Most do, and honestly, eventually I'd love to. But my actual pitch is: use my help free until you don't need it. Worst case you sell your house with better photos."
Play it straight: if a FSBO is listed with limited-service MLS entry, check your MLS rules on solicitation. Never misrepresent "having a buyer" to get in the door — if you claim buyer interest, it must be real. Trust is the entire strategy here.
