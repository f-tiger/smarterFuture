How to Get More Real Estate Reviews (Scripts + Timing)

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

Reviews are the modern referral: strangers trust them, Google ranks you by them, and every closed deal can produce one — if you ask at the right moment, in the right way. Most agents ask too late, too vaguely, or not at all. Here's the system.

When to ask (timing is everything)

  • The emotional peak: key handoff for buyers, accepted-offer or closing day for sellers. Gratitude fades roughly by half every week after closing.
  • After a save: any moment a client says "thank you so much" mid-transaction — that's an invitation. "You just made my day — would you be up for saying that in a review when we close? I'll send the link." Pre-seeding doubles your hit rate.

The ask, word for word

Text (best response rate): "It was genuinely a joy helping you get [Address], [First name]. If you have 90 seconds, a quick review is the single best way my next client finds me: [direct link]. Even two sentences about what the process felt like helps more than you'd think."

Email version: same message plus one nudge of specificity: "If it helps, people often mention how [the thing you actually did — negotiation, communication, calming the chaos] went." You're not scripting them; you're rescuing them from the blank box — the #1 reason people don't finish reviews.

The gentle chase (one week later, once): "No pressure at all on the review — I know life after moving is chaos. Here's the link again in case it got buried: [link]. Either way, enjoy the new place!"

Where to send them

Priority order: Google (feeds the map pack), then Zillow (where buyers already are), then Facebook. One platform per client — don't ask anyone to post twice. Rotate by where each client "lives" online.

Handling a bad review

  • Reply once, publicly, calmly, within 48 hours: acknowledge, state your side in one factual sentence without revealing private details, invite the offline conversation. You're writing for the hundreds of future readers, not the one reviewer.
  • Never argue, never beg for removal in public, and know the platform rules: you can flag reviews that violate policy (fake, wrong business), but "unfair" isn't a violation.
  • The real fix is volume: one sour note in thirty reviews reads as noise; in three reviews it reads as a pattern.
Play it clean: no paying, no discounts-for-reviews, no review gating (only asking happy clients through a filter tool), no writing them yourself. Platforms detect it, regulators fine it, and one exposed fake costs more trust than fifty real reviews earn.

Reuse every testimonial

A review's second life is marketing: with permission, quote it in your listing presentation, your newsletter, and social posts (the content guide has formats). Our free AI tools can turn a long review into a tight social caption in one paste.
