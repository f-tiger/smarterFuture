Buyer Consultation Questions That Turn Lookers Into Clients

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

The buyer consultation is where casual lookers become committed clients — or where you sign up for three months of unpaid chauffeuring. The difference is almost entirely the questions you ask in the first meeting. Here's a 30-minute structure and the exact questions, in the order that works.

Open with motivation (5 minutes)

  • "What's making you think about moving right now?" — the answer tells you urgency, emotion, and timeline in one shot.
  • "What happens if you don't find a place this year?" — buyers with a real consequence ("our lease ends in June") behave very differently from "we'd just keep renting."
  • "Have you bought a home before? What went well — and what would you do differently this time?"

Money before must-haves (10 minutes)

Houses first, money later wastes everyone's time. Ask it plainly and without apology:

  • "Have you talked with a lender yet? Are you pre-qualified or fully pre-approved?" (Explain the difference — sellers take pre-approval seriously.)
  • "What monthly payment feels comfortable — not the max a lender approves, the number you'd be happy writing every month?"
  • "Beyond the down payment, are you set for closing costs and a reserve? I'd rather size this honestly now than have a surprise at week five."

Must-haves vs. nice-to-haves (10 minutes)

  • "Give me your top three non-negotiables — the things a home simply must have."
  • "Now the wish list — what would make you smile but wouldn't kill the deal?"
  • "What did you hate about the last place you lived?" (Often more revealing than what they want.)
  • "Who else weighs in on this decision — partner, parents, anyone whose opinion could change things at the last minute?" (Find the hidden decision-maker now.)
Fair-housing note: steer by property features and budget, never by the demographics of a neighborhood. If a buyer asks "is this a good area for families like ours?", answer with objective data sources — schools' published info, crime statistics portals — not your characterization. The fair-housing guide covers the language to avoid.

Set the working agreement (5 minutes)

  • "Here's how I work: I'll send new matches the hour they list, preview when it's useful, and tell you honestly when a house is overpriced — even if it costs me a faster commission. In return I ask that we're straight with each other: if a house isn't right, say so and say why."
  • "On timing: when you find the house, this market rewards buyers who can move in [X hours/days]. Let's agree now on how we'll make decisions fast without feeling rushed."

After the meeting

Send a same-day recap: their top three, budget frame, and the two homes you're lining up. It proves you listened and creates the paper trail that keeps everyone honest.
