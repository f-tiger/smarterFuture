How to Write a Listing Description From Photos (When You're Short on Facts)

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

You're standing in the home with your phone, the seller is out, and you don't have the full spec sheet yet — just what you can see. This happens more than agents like to admit, and it's exactly when AI-written descriptions go wrong: a model asked to "describe this listing" from a photo alone will happily invent square footage, room counts, and upgrades it can't actually verify. Here's how to get a usable draft without that risk.

Step 1: Separate what you can see from what you can't

Walk your photo set and sort details into two lists:

  • Visually verifiable — flooring type, counter material, natural light, layout flow between rooms, exterior style, yard condition, visible age of finishes
  • Not verifiable from photos — exact square footage, lot size, year built, HVAC age, school district, recent permits or renovations

Only the first list goes into your description draft. The second list needs the MLS data sheet, tax records, or a quick text to the listing agent or seller before you publish — even if that means the description goes out a day later.

Step 2: Describe features, not guesses

"Kitchen has quartz-look counters" is safe if you can see the material; "recently renovated kitchen" is a guess unless you know the renovation date. Stick to what's observable:

  • Instead of "updated bathroom" → "bathroom with a walk-in glass shower and double vanity"
  • Instead of "spacious primary suite" → "primary bedroom with room for a king bed and a sitting area, plus an en-suite bath"
  • Instead of "move-in ready" (a claim, not a fact) → describe the specific condition you can see: fresh paint, refinished floors, new fixtures

Step 3: Use a photo-grounded prompt, not a generic one

If you're using an AI tool, be explicit about the constraint. A prompt like this keeps the model honest:

Copy-paste prompt template:

  • You are a real estate copywriter. I'm giving you a list of visually observed features only — no square footage, year built, or history is confirmed yet.
  • Observed features: [your list from Step 1].
  • Write a 100-120 word teaser description using only these features. Do not state or imply square footage, bed/bath count, age, or school district unless I explicitly provide them. Flag with [CONFIRM] anywhere I should double-check a fact before publishing.
Tip: This "teaser" version is genuinely useful even after you get the full spec sheet — it's exactly the kind of short, visual copy that works for a social caption or a coming-soon post. See AI social captions for realtors.

Step 4: Upgrade to the full description once facts arrive

Treat the photo-only draft as a first pass, not the final copy. Once you have the real spec sheet, run it through the full fact-checklist workflow in how to write a listing with AI — or paste your confirmed facts into our free Listing Description Generator — and merge in the verified numbers before it goes live on the MLS.

Why this matters more than it seems

An inflated or invented detail in an MLS description isn't just embarrassing when a buyer notices during a showing — it can become a disclosure or misrepresentation issue depending on your state. Photos are a great starting point for tone and feel; they are never a substitute for confirmed facts.
