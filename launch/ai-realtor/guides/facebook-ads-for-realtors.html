Facebook Ads for Realtors: A Beginner's Playbook

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

Facebook (and Instagram, run from the same Ads Manager) is where most agents get their first paid leads — but it's also where most agents quietly waste a few hundred dollars learning what not to do. This playbook keeps you on the useful side of that line: what to run first, how to target without wasting reach, what to budget, and the mistakes that burn cash. Note upfront: housing ads run under Meta's Special Ad Category, which restricts some targeting for fair-housing reasons — that's a feature, not a bug, and this guide works within it.

Run this first: a single listing or lead-magnet ad

Don't start with a complex funnel. Start with one clear objective:

  • Listing ad — promote one attractive, well-photographed listing to your local area. Objective: "Leads" or "Traffic" to the listing page. Great for seller impressions and buyer inquiries.
  • Lead-magnet ad — offer something genuinely useful (a "what's my home worth" consult, a free buyer's guide, a neighborhood market update) in exchange for a name and email. This builds a list you own instead of renting attention.

Pick one. Get it working before you add a second.

Targeting under the Special Ad Category

For real estate, Meta removes age, gender, ZIP-code, and many interest targets to prevent discrimination. What you still have is plenty:

  • A broad location radius (Meta requires a minimum 15-mile radius for housing) around your market
  • Lookalike audiences built from your past client list or website visitors — the single most effective targeting lever left
  • Retargeting people who visited your site or engaged with your page

Let Meta's algorithm optimize within a broad audience rather than over-narrowing. With the restrictions in place, broad + a strong creative usually beats clever targeting.

What to budget (and how to not panic)

Start small enough that learning is cheap: many agents begin at $5–$10/day for 5–7 days per test. That's enough for Meta to exit the "learning phase" and show you real cost-per-lead. Judge results on cost per qualified lead, not raw clicks — 3 good leads beat 30 tire-kickers. Kill or tweak anything that isn't producing after the test window; scale only what works.

Tip: The creative matters more than the budget. A scroll-stopping first image/video and a clear, honest offer will out-perform a bigger spend on a boring ad every time. Write several ad variations and let Meta test them — our free caption generator and AI tools can draft variations fast.

The mistakes that waste money

  • Boosting a post instead of using Ads Manager. Boosts are the blunt, expensive version. Use Ads Manager for real objectives and audiences.
  • Sending clicks to your homepage. Send them to a focused landing page or lead form about the exact thing the ad promised.
  • No follow-up system. A lead that isn't contacted within minutes goes cold. Have your speed-to-lead system ready before you spend a dollar.
  • Judging too early. Let a test run its full window before deciding. Turning ads on and off daily resets Meta's learning.
  • Fair-housing-risky copy. The same rules that govern listings govern ads — describe the home and the offer, not who should respond. See fair-housing compliance.

Facebook ads reward patience and a good offer, not big budgets. Start with one ad, one clear objective, a small test budget, and a follow-up system that's actually ready — then scale the winners. For the organic side that makes your paid ads convert better, see what to post on social media.
