Buyer Objection Scripts: What to Say When They Hesitate

ListingLift · Practical AI for real-estate agents

Buyer objections are rarely arguments to win — they're anxieties to address. The buyer saying "we want to wait" is really saying "we're scared of overpaying." Answer the fear underneath and the objection resolves itself. Scripts for the big five:

"We want to wait for prices to come down."

  • "Totally reasonable instinct — let's put numbers on it. If prices dip [X]% but rates rise [Y]%, your monthly payment goes up, not down. And the inverse: if rates fall, prices usually jump because everyone comes back at once. Rather than betting on the market, can we define what a fair price for the right house looks like today? You can always refinance a rate; you can't refinance a purchase price — so let's make sure the price is right and the house is worth owning either way."

"We want to see more houses first."

  • "Of course — and one honest question so I can help instead of chauffeur: is it that none of these felt right, or that this one felt right and that's scary? Because those need opposite plans. If the list is wrong, let's fix the list tonight. If it's the second one… that feeling of 'too fast' is what finding it feels like in this market. What would you need to know to feel as confident as the situation deserves?"

"Let's offer way under asking and see."

  • "We can absolutely open below ask when the data supports it — here's what the comps say this one's actually worth: [number]. A lowball on a fairly-priced home doesn't start a negotiation; it usually ends one, and if another offer lands while we're playing games, we've lost your leverage entirely. Let's decide your true walk-away number first, then structure the opening offer to get there."

"Why do we need you? / What about your fee?"

  • "Fair question, and I'd rather answer it plainly than dodge: here's exactly what I do — [pricing analysis, negotiation, inspection strategy, vendor bench, contract-to-close management] — and here's how my compensation works in this deal: [explain your agreement clearly]. If at any point you feel you're not getting more than that in value, tell me directly." (Buyer-agreement rules changed recently — know your local requirements and put compensation in writing up front.)

Cold feet after the accepted offer

  • "What you're feeling has a name — buyer's remorse hits almost everyone about 48 hours in, on every price point. Let's separate signal from noise: is anything factually different from when you offered? If yes, we have contingencies and we'll use them without hesitation. If no — remember the list you gave me at our first meeting? This house hit [X] of your top [Y]. The house didn't change; the adrenaline did."
The meta-skill: validate first, data second, decision framework third. Never "overcome" an objection with pressure — buyers who feel pushed become the deals that die in escrow. And every claim you make (comps, market direction) must be true and sourced; confidence built on accurate data is the whole job.
